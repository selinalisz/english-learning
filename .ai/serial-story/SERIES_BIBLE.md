## Series Title

The Blue Receipt

## Core Premise

Mina, a Taiwanese woman trying to use English more bravely, starts receiving strange English messages on everyday receipts, signs, and screens. Each message warns her about a small choice she is about to make. At first the messages look like mistakes, but they slowly point to a hidden pattern around buses, stations, cafes, and one person who seems to know where she will go next.

## Original Learning Goal, Reframed

The story should make the learner want to read the next episode first. The English learning value comes through the story naturally:

- Travel communication: asking for help, checking routes, handling stations, hotels, bags, tickets, and timing.
- Online / public English reading: receipts, signs, app messages, maps, warnings, instructions, schedules, and search results.
- Daily spoken English: short questions, clear replies, and useful phrases that a learner can say in real life.

## Main Character

- Name: Mina Lin
- Native language: Traditional Chinese
- English level in story: A2-B1, brave but not fluent
- External goal: reach places, solve small real-life tasks, and understand the messages
- Internal goal: stop freezing when English appears in real life
- Fear: choosing the wrong route, misunderstanding a warning, looking foolish when asking
- Strength: careful observation; she notices small details others miss
- Voice: practical, curious, slightly nervous, but growing steadier

## Recurring Characters

- The cafe worker: calm, cautious, may know more than he says
- The blue bus driver: appears at key moments; not clearly a villain or helper yet
- Lina: Mina's friend, reachable by messages; brings warmth and common sense
- The unknown sender: leaves English messages in public systems; identity unknown

## Story Engine

Each episode should include:

- One ordinary real-life task Mina needs to complete
- One English clue, warning, sign, message, receipt, app screen, or short dialogue
- One moment where Mina must understand or say English to move forward
- One emotional beat: worry, relief, curiosity, doubt, or courage
- One small hook at the end, unless the episode is a planned recap or resolution

## Difficulty

- Target: A2 moving toward B1
- Story paragraph length: usually 130-180 English words
- Sentence length: mostly under 12 words; occasional 13-15 word sentence is allowed for story flow
- Vocabulary: mostly high-frequency words; new words must be useful outside the story
- Tone: simple suspense, not horror; no graphic violence
- Avoid: textbook explanation inside the story, unnatural vocabulary stuffing, long exposition

## Learning Structure

The article becomes the next story episode. The lower learning sections stay in the existing format:

- New Words Today
- Key Phrases
- Quiz
- Active Recall Quiz
- Speaking Bridge
- Learning Tips
- Review Words
- Article audio and sentence audio

## Continuity Rules

- Before writing a new episode, read `CONTINUITY_LOG.md`.
- Continue from the latest episode unless the user explicitly asks for a reset.
- Do not resolve the main mystery too early.
- Track every clue that needs later payoff.
- If a detail changes, update the continuity log and explain why.
